The project

Hollowfen — The Failing Village.

A third-person foraging game. You play Wren, a young mushroom-identifier who returns to a damp, half-abandoned medieval village and slowly rebuilds it by learning to read the forest — which mushrooms feed people, and which kill them.

The mechanic that makes it work

Claude reads & writes the Unity editor directly.

I describe what I want in words. Claude inspects the open scene over the MCP server, places assets, wires up components, and reads the console back — then tells me what changed.

I stay the director. It does the editor work.

What got built · 01 · The world

A village you can
actually walk.

Built from a purchased Medieval Fantasy Town Village pack — individual building and prop prefabs dropped into a hand-authored layout, with collision walls and punched-out doorways so Wren moves through it, not into it.

The damp, failing mood comes from low fog and a driven sky — overcast by day, warm lanterns creeping in at dusk.

I said: “Give me a damp, lived-in medieval village — not a fantasy theme park.”

What got built · 02 · The character
[ SCREENSHOT ]
Wren close-up + the animation states: idle · walk · run · jump.

Wren, the forager.

The character was generated in Meshy, then brought in with a full animation set — idle, walk, run, jump — driven by a third-person controller: WASD to move, shift to run, space to jump, drag to turn the camera.

Height-normalized and re-lit so she sits inside the world instead of on top of it.

I said: “A young woman with a forager's satchel and practical clothes — she studies mushrooms, she doesn't fight.”

What got built · 03 · The core loop

The forage-and-identify loop.

16 mushroom species, each with real field-guide data — Latin name, edibility, ID features, habitat, season, and the toxic look-alikes that make it dangerous.

You find one, read the cues, and decide. Get it right, it enters your guide. Misread a Death Cap for a Field Mushroom — and that's the whole tension.

06 / 09
Plain language → scene

What I said vs. what appeared.

I said

“The village should feel damp and half-abandoned — overcast light, fog sitting low, and warmer lanterns as it gets toward dusk.”

What appeared

A driven sky + fog system with a time-of-day control — dawn → noon → dusk → night. Sun intensity, sky haze, fog colour and exposure all shift with the hour; the fog warms at dusk and goes blue-black by night.

dawn noon dusk night

The gap between those two boxes is the whole skill: say it precisely enough, and the scene is what comes back.

07 / 09
What this taught me

The win isn't the tool. It's knowing
where the line sits.

01

Fast & literal in the editor. A clear, bounded ask gets placed, wired and checked faster than I could by hand.

02

Not a designer. “Make it good” gives mush. “Fog low, light warm at dusk” gives the scene.

03

My job moved from doing the clicks to holding the vision, breaking it down, and judging the result.

AI enablement, for me, is two kinds of specific: specific about intent — and specific about where the model stops and I start.
